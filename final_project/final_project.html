Which Goods and Services Drove Inflation During COVID-19?

A peak into PCE inflation goods and services during the pandemic

The onset of the COVID-19 pandemic threw our way of life into a panic. The manner in which we lived seemingly changed overnight. The mass closures for the good of public health lead to a short, but deep recession, one of the largest shocks to our economy in history led to price pressures that have not been seen in decades. Inflation went from a topic only discussed by academics and policymakers to being on the forefront of public debate. Consumers now find themselves painfully aware of inflation. A fair question to ask would be "which of the goods and services have been driving the high levels of inflation"? This project aims to shed a little light on that quesiton.

Taking a step back-what is inflation? Why is this question important to us? Inflation can be characterized as the general increase in the overall price level of the goods and services in the economy (cite). It's importance to our day-to-day lives cannot be understated. Inflation affects the purchasing power that people have first and foremost, but also has been shown to be a key indicator in both expected and realized economic performance. Specific to this project, the measure of inflation I use is the Bureau of Economic Analysis' (BEA) Personal Consumption Expenditures Price Index (PCE). This is the measure targeted by the Federal Reserve in the United States, and is "A measure of the prices that people living in the United States, or those buying on their behalf, pay for goods and services" (cite). PCE uses 201 goods and services to approximate the general change in price level for consumers. Each month, a representative sample of consumers are surveyed about what they purchase, both the price and quantity. The BEA then tabulates each goods price and quantity for consumers. To compute the reported "headline" inflation rate, the expenditure weighted average (as determined by the quantities) of the price changes is computed. Similarly, different baskets can be computed ("core" inflation is a commonly reported one-all goods less energy and food items). These aggregate metrics can be a good indicator of the general trend in prices, but may obfiscate underlying trends in specific goods.

How much did some of the most volatile components change during COVID-19?

Source: Authors' calculations via Bureau of Economic Analysis

The charts above look at five of the most volatile goods and services in terms of monthly price changes in the PCE price index during the COVID-19 period. This illustrates just how extreme the largest deviation from February 2020 price levels were (marked "Max Deviation"), and what the current price is in relation to the pre-COVID period ("Current"). Although each of these goods are the most volatile in terms of price changes, the nature of those changes are quite different. Gasoline, and other fuel/oil behaved in a predictable pattern. Peaking in price during 2021 only to come down, but not to pre-COVID levels. Film/photo supplies and primary school lunches took a different shape, however. The pandemic caused a rapid decrease in prices for consumers for both goods (the school lunches have an intuitive answer as to why, many free-lunch programs were instituted at the beginning of the pandemic, causing consumer spending on school lunches to drop).

COVID-19 led to massive price swings

Source: Authors' calculations via Bureau of Economic Analysis

This variable area chart shows the 10 highest weighted goods on average during the COVID-19 period. The thickness of the line denotes the weighted-price index for the good, with the lines towards the top denoting the goods with the higher index value (February 2020=100). It can be seen that the initial wave of COVID-19 (2020) led to rapid swings in the prices of these goods. However, by mid-2021, the change in the price indices smoothed out, although many at the top (energy goods in particular) have continued to grow in their index value, which can be seen by the growing width of the top few lines.
