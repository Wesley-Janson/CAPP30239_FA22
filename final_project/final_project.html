Which Goods and Services Drove Inflation During COVID-19?

A peek into PCE inflation components during the pandemic

The onset of the COVID-19 pandemic threw our way of life into a panic. The manner in which we lived seemingly changed overnight. The mass closures for the good of public health led to a short, but deep recession, one of the largest shocks to our economy in history led to price pressures that have not been seen in decades. Inflation went from a topic only discussed by academics and policymakers to being on the forefront of public debate. Consumers now find themselves painfully aware of inflation. A fair question to ask would be "which of the goods and services have been driving the high levels of inflation"? This project aims to shed a little light on that question.

Taking a step back-what is inflation? Why is this question important to us? Inflation can be characterized as the general increase in the overall price level of the goods and services in the economy (Federal Reserve). It's importance to our day-to-day lives cannot be understated. Inflation affects the purchasing power that people have first and foremost, but also has been shown to be a key indicator in both expected and realized economic performance. Specific to this project, the measure of inflation I use is the Bureau of Economic Analysis' (BEA) Personal Consumption Expenditures Price Index (PCE). This is the measure targeted by the Federal Reserve in the United States and is "A measure of the prices that people living in the United States, or those buying on their behalf, pay for goods and services" (BEA).

PCE inflation uses 201 goods and services to approximate the general change in price level for consumers. Each month, a representative sample of consumers and businesses are surveyed about what they purchase, both the price and quantity. The BEA then tabulates each goods price and quantity for consumers. To compute the reported "headline" inflation rate, the expenditure weighted average (as determined by the quantities) of the price changes is computed. Similarly, different baskets can be computed ("core" inflation is a commonly reported one-all goods less energy and food items). These aggregate metrics can be a good indicator of the general trend in prices but may obfuscate underlying trends in specific goods. It was with that in mind that I set out to explore the components that make up PCE inflation, and how their prices and weights have changed during COVID-19.

Not all price changes are created equal

Source: Authors' calculations via Bureau of Economic Analysis

Among all the goods and services that are used to compute PCE inflation, we see the price changes vary widely during the COVID-19 period. It is noteworthy that the very bottom and top of the distribution are considerably distant from the average and median percent change in prices. However, everything between the 25th and 75th percentiles are relatively stable and consistent.

A question that a reasonable person could ask is: if we are taking the average of the price changes to create the inflation rate, shouldn't these extreme percentiles greatly effect it? While on the right path in recognizing that averages are susceptible to outliers, it is the expenditure weights that go into the creation of inflation that largely prevent these outlier components from pulling the inflation measure one way or another. But what happens when these high-weight goods change dramatically? The short answer: the inflation measure follows suit. The next section shows how our current inflationary episode is being influenced by the highest weighted components.

COVID-19 led to massive price swings in the highest weighted goods

Source: Authors' calculations via Bureau of Economic Analysis

This variable area chart shows the 10 highest weighted goods on average during the COVID-19 period. The thickness of the line denotes the weighted-price index for the good, with the lines towards the top denoting the goods with the higher index value (February 2020=100). Each good begins at 100 on the leftmost side of the graph, and the subsequent months the goods are ranked by their index value. The initial wave of COVID-19 (2020) led to rapid swings in the prices of these goods. However, by mid-2021, the change in the price indices smoothed out, although many at the top (energy goods in particular) continued to grow in their index value, which can be seen by the growing width of the top few lines. Only recently have the prices in the energy sector started to cool off but remain well above pre-pandemic levels.

How much did some of the most volatile components change during COVID-19?

Source: Authors' calculations via Bureau of Economic Analysis

The charts above look at five of the most volatile goods and services in terms of monthly price changes in the PCE price index during the COVID-19 period. This illustrates just how extreme the largest deviation from February 2020 price levels were (marked "Max Deviation"), and what the current price is in relation to the pre-COVID period ("Current"). It can be interpreted as follows: the size of both the "Current" and "Max Deviation" circles are directly proportional to the "Feb 2020" circle in terms of index value. For example, an index value of 150 would correspond to a circle 50% larger than the Feb 2020 circle. Although each of these goods are the most volatile in terms of price changes, the nature of those changes is quite different. Gasoline, and other fuel/oil behaved in a predictable pattern. Peaking in price during 2021 only to come down, but not to pre-COVID levels. Film/photo supplies and primary school lunches took a different shape, however. The pandemic caused a rapid decrease in prices for consumers for both goods (school lunches have an intuitive answer as to why, as students in primary schools across the country were sent home to learn, spending on school lunches quite obviously dropped in tandem).

Process

This topic was of interest to me due to my research background on the topic. Before entering the CAPP program, I was a Research Analyst at the Federal Reserve Bank of Cleveland where I predominantly researched topics related to inflation dynamics and expectations. For most of my time there, the topic of inflation was not one of public interest in the U.S., as price levels had been relatively calm for close to four decades. With the onset of COVID-19, that changed. The initial shock to the economy was then followed by levels of inflation not seen in the U.S. since the early 1980s. With the topic now much more of interest to the general public, I thought using some of my previous knowledge could lead to an interesting project.

Using PCE instead of another measure of inflation (namely CPI) had more to do with personal bias than anything. Having previous work PCE gave me some level of familiarity with the data that I don't have with CPI. Though there are some subtle differences between PCE and CPI (a short publication here can help inform on those), much of the same narrative could be spun with an alternate measure. The raw price index and quantities data are publicly available at the BEA's website. However, I was able to gain access to an intermediate dataset that the Federal Reserve Bank of Cleveland uses to create their Median PCE Inflation bank product. This is the same data as the BEA, but in a format where the quantities of each good in a given month is expressed as the weight. With the data in a digestible format, the data cleaning was not all that difficult. The more arduous task was to narrow down which data in specific to use to answer the question "which goods and services drove inflation during COVID-19?". I decided the narrative I wanted to tell was that there are two main inputs into inflation: prices and weights. Understanding how these two interact is paramount in answering the question posed in the title of this project.

My first chart sets up the story that each good or service in PCE may have wildly different price changes in any given month. But as I mention, simply observing the price changes without accounting for the weights is quite deceptive. If one good has a price change 10 percentage points higher than the average that certainly is of note, but if it comprises just a small portion of the "basket" of goods, its overall importance to inflation is quite negligible. The idea of the importance of weights leads me to my second chart, which looks at the highest weighted goods seen during the COVID-19 period (as determined by average monthly weight from March 2020-September 2022) and look at how their price change patterns developed over time. This visual was designed to allow one to easily see how at one point during the pandemic, the highest weighted (in some respects, most important) goods saw extreme price changes in relation to pre-COVID levels, and though now are less extreme, still have not yet returned to February 2020 levels. In juxtaposition to weights, my last chart emphasizes the large changes in prices during the COVID-19 period. By taking the 10 goods with the highest volatility in price from March 2020-September 2022 (as defined by their variance), I can see the extent to which the global pandemic and the ensuing supply chain issues had. One noteworthy observation, there is a bit of overlap between the goods that appear in the second and third figures. These are the exact goods that should receive attention-goods that are relatively large purchases for consumers that experienced large price swings.

I was rather fortunate to have data easily accessible and in a "clean" format, so the only real limitation I came across was attempting a D3.js figure that is maybe more complicated than my current skillset allows for. However, throughout this project, I feel I was able to gain a much better grasp of D3.js, and now have more confidence in my abilities to problem solve problems with future visualizations.
